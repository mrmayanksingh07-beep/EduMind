Thermodynamics
NCERT Class 11 Physics Chapter 12 ⏱ ~35 min read

12.1 Introduction

Thermodynamics is a branch of physics that deals with heat, work, and temperature, and their relation to energy and entropy. The behavior of these quantities is governed by the four laws of thermodynamics, irrespective of the composition or specific properties of the material or system.

The word thermodynamics comes from the Greek words therme (heat) and dynamis (power). A thermodynamic system is a definite macroscopic region of the universe under study.

12.2 Thermal Equilibrium

Two systems are in thermal equilibrium if they have the same temperature and no heat flows between them when brought into contact. This is the basis of temperature measurement itself.

⚛️ Zeroth Law: If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B is in equilibrium with C, then A and C are also in thermal equilibrium.

12.3 Heat, Internal Energy & Work

Internal Energy (U) is the total energy of all molecules in a system — kinetic + potential. Heat (Q) is energy transferred due to temperature difference. Work (W) is energy transferred due to force.

The First Law of Thermodynamics is essentially the law of conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems. It states that the change in internal energy equals the heat added minus the work done by the system.

📐 First Law: ΔU = Q − W  |  where ΔU = change in internal energy, Q = heat added, W = work done by system

12.4 Thermodynamic Processes

In an isothermal process, temperature remains constant (ΔT = 0, ΔU = 0). In an adiabatic process, no heat is exchanged with surroundings (Q = 0). In an isochoric process, volume is constant (W = 0). In an isobaric process, pressure is constant.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body. Entropy of an isolated system always increases or remains constant — it never decreases.

🔄 Entropy: ΔS = Q/T  |  For any spontaneous process in an isolated system: ΔS ≥ 0

12.5 Heat Engines & Efficiency

A heat engine converts heat energy into mechanical work. It takes heat Q₁ from a hot reservoir at temperature T₁, converts part of it to work W, and rejects the remainder Q₂ to a cold reservoir at temperature T₂.

⚙️ Efficiency: η = W/Q₁ = 1 − (Q₂/Q₁) = 1 − (T₂/T₁)   [Carnot Efficiency]

What is the difference between isothermal and adiabatic?
🤖
Great question! Simple way to remember:

Isothermal = Same Temperature
→ Temperature stays constant (ΔT = 0)
→ Happens slowly, heat can exchange with surroundings

Adiabatic = No Heat Transfer
→ No heat enters or leaves (Q = 0)
→ Happens very fast (like compression in a cycle)

Think of it this way: iso = "same", adiabatic = "no heat allowed in or out" 🎯
